NRG Energy Solution: Centralised Solar Power System for Saibai Island

A sustainable, community-focused renewable energy solution addressing diesel dependency in remote Torres Strait communities

Quick Facts

  • Design Area: 4.1 Clean, affordable energy for small coastal urban environments
  • Project Team: Yusuf Bharoocha, Nicholas Limpiado, Jinchul Park, Saumya Pindoriya, Sean Woods

Proposal Summary

This project addresses Saibai Island's critical energy challenges by replacing diesel-dependent power generation with a centralised solar farm and battery storage system. The solution achieves $172,780 annual savings, eliminates 500 tonnes of CO‚ÇÇ emissions yearly, and creates local employment opportunities.

Executive Summary

Overview of Design

  • 82 x 6.6kW solar systems providing ~400kW total capacity
  • Generates ~2200kWh daily to meet island's full energy demand
  • Containerized Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) with 500kWh+ capacity
  • Marine-grade, cyclone-rated materials for harsh coastal environment
  • Single centralised location (OT1 site) for efficient maintenance
  • Serves 77 households (340 residents)
  • Diesel generators retained as backup

Project Background

Introduction

Saibai Island faces an urgent energy crisis. Located just 4km from Papua New Guinea and 150km north of mainland Australia, this low-lying Torres Strait island community of 340 people relies almost entirely on diesel generators for power‚Äîa situation that is economically unsustainable, environmentally destructive, and culturally at odds with the community's values as custodians of their land and sea.

Saibai Island Context

Location

115km¬≤ low-lying mud island, highest point only 1.7m above sea level

Population

340 residents in 77 households

Energy Demand

~2000kWh daily consumption

Current Source

95% diesel generation

The Diesel Problem

üí∞ Financial Burden

  • Diesel cost: $3.70/L (vs ~$1.80/L mainland)
  • 150km sea transport via barge drives costs
  • Current energy cost: 30 cents/kWh
  • Annual expenditure unsustainable for community budget

‚ö†Ô∏è Supply Vulnerability

  • Monsoon seasons (Nov-April) disrupt barge deliveries
  • Weather delays can leave community without backup fuel
  • Power station and fuel station vulnerable to extreme weather
  • Critical infrastructure depends on unreliable supply chain

üîß Maintenance Crisis

  • Salt corrosion destroys diesel equipment rapidly
  • No qualified diesel technicians on island
  • Repairs require expensive mainland callouts
  • Frequent breakdowns and power cuts

üåø Environmental Impact

  • ~500 tonnes CO‚ÇÇ emissions annually
  • Diesel fumes affect community health and quality of life
  • Noise pollution from generators
  • Fossil fuel dependency conflicts with cultural values and climate reality

The Economic Reality

Torres Strait Island Regional Council (TSIRC) Budget:

  • Operating Revenue: $81 million
  • Operating Expenses: $141 million
  • Annual Deficit: $60 million
  • 53.81% of income from government grants

Council must prioritize essential services (health, education) over energy infrastructure

With TSIRC operating at such a significant deficit, any energy solution must demonstrably reduce the financial burden while delivering long-term value to the community.

Logistical Challenges

Why Not Air Freight?

  • Only transports perishable items
  • Less cost-effective than barge
  • Aerodrome lacks consistent communications
  • Extreme weather causes delays

Solution: Sea Swift barge service provides reliable, cost-effective transport for large equipment from mainland Queensland.

Design Criteria

Economically Viable

Description

The solution must provide long-term cost savings compared to diesel generation while remaining within feasible implementation budgets for TSIRC. This includes capital costs, operational expenses, and maintenance over 25+ year lifespan.

Justification

With TSIRC operating at $60M annual deficit, any energy solution must reduce financial burden while delivering community value. Current diesel costs are unsustainable.

Community Impact

Lower energy costs mean more council resources available for essential services like healthcare, education, and infrastructure.

Reliable

Description

System must provide consistent 24/7 power despite challenging environmental conditions including monsoons, king tides, salt spray, and cyclones. Must include adequate battery storage and backup systems.

Justification

Community wellbeing depends on uninterrupted power for refrigeration, medical equipment, water pumping, communications, and daily living. Current diesel system's supply vulnerability is unacceptable.

Community Impact

Families can rely on consistent power for food storage, children's education (lighting for homework), and critical health needs without fear of outages.

Environmentally Sustainable

Description

Solution must minimize carbon emissions, reduce fossil fuel dependency, and align with Torres Strait Islander values of environmental stewardship. Should help community adapt to climate change impacts.

Justification

Saibai Island faces existential threats from sea level rise due to climate change. The community has been clear: they want solutions that protect their homeland, not contribute to its destruction.

Community Impact

Eliminating 500 tonnes annual CO‚ÇÇ emissions demonstrates community leadership in climate action and reduces local air and noise pollution affecting daily life.

Maintainable

Description

System must be serviceable by locally trained technicians without requiring specialized mainland expertise for routine operations. Spare parts should be accessible and maintenance procedures straightforward.

Justification

Remote location makes mainland technician callouts expensive (travel, accommodation) and time-consuming. Community needs ability to manage their own infrastructure.

Community Impact

Creates local employment opportunities, builds community capacity, ensures rapid response to any issues without waiting for external help.

Accessible and Scalable

Description

Solution must be implementable using available transport infrastructure (barge delivery) and scalable to other Torres Strait islands with similar challenges. Technology should be proven and widely supported.

Justification

Saibai's success can become model for 15+ other remote Torres Strait communities facing identical energy challenges. Solutions must work within existing logistics constraints.

Community Impact

Demonstrates feasible pathway for entire Torres Strait region to achieve energy independence, with Saibai residents potentially training technicians from neighboring islands.

Our Solution

Centralised Solar + Battery + Grid Integration

A comprehensive renewable energy system designed specifically for Saibai Island's unique challenges

Technical Specifications

‚òÄÔ∏è Solar Array

  • Size: 82 x 6.6kW systems = ~400kW total capacity (ground-mounted)
  • Power Generation: ~2200kWh per day to meet full island demand
  • Technology: High-efficiency monocrystalline panels (best for marine environments)
  • Sunlight: ~5.5 hours average daily sunlight powers the island
  • Installation: Marine-grade, corrosion-resistant materials
  • Resilience: Cyclone-rated mounting systems for extreme weather protection

üîã Battery Storage (BESS)

  • Type: Centralised, Containerized Battery Energy Storage System
  • Capacity: 500kWh+ (sized for full overnight power supply)
  • Purpose: 24/7 power availability + 2-3 days backup during extended cloud cover
  • Technology: Lithium-ion, containerized for easy barge transport and installation
  • Benefit: Eliminates need for diesel generators except in extreme emergencies

‚ö° Grid Integration

  • Connection: Direct integration with Saibai's existing 3-phase electrical infrastructure
  • Role: Solar/battery provides primary power; diesel generators remain as backup only
  • System: Inverter and transformer manage power conversion and distribution
  • Benefit: Utilizes existing distribution network‚Äîno need to rewire 77 households
